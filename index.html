IDEATE

Connecting the Public Sector with their Users

Design Challenge

How might we connect Irish public-sector decision-makers with the users of their services so that they can imagine and create more useful and desirable digital public services?

Before attempting to connect public service decision-makers with users, it is essential to first understand the barriers that users must overcome to engage in the process. Understanding these barriers is crucial for developing effective strategies to promote accessibility, inclusivity, and meaningful engagement between decision-makers and the public. By identifying and addressing these barriers, we can create more equitable and user-centered approaches to public service interactions.

Barriers / Pain Points

Language Barriers

Many refugees won’t speak the language of their host country fluently, making it difficult for them to understand and participate in decision-making processes related to public housing.

Lack of Trust

Some refugees may have experienced trauma or discrimination in their home countries or during their journey to the host country, leading to a lack of trust in government officials and decision-makers.

Cultural Barriers

Refugees may come from different cultural backgrounds with different values and beliefs, which can make it challenging for decision-makers to understand their needs and preferences.

Limited access to information

Refugees may not have access to the same information channels as other citizens, such as social media or local news outlets, making it difficult for them to stay informed about public housing decisions.

Economic barriers

Many refugees may need help with financial challenges, such as unemployment or low income, which can limit their ability to participate in decision-making processes that require time and resources.

Lack of awareness

Decision-makers may not be aware of the specific needs and challenges faced by refugees when it comes to public housing, leading to inadequate policies and programs.

Legal barriers

Refugees may face legal barriers to accessing public housing services, such as restrictions on eligibility or residency requirements.

Discrimination

Some refugees may face discrimination from public service decision-makers, which can make it harder for them to access the services they need.

Cultural Differences

Cultural differences between refugees and public service decision-makers can create barriers to communication and understanding, making it harder for refugees to access the services they need.

Lack of Information

Refugees may not be aware of the public housing services available to them or how to access them, leading to a lack of knowledge about their options.

Financial Barriers

Refugees may struggle to afford the costs associated with accessing public housing services, such as application fees or security deposits

Lack of social support

Refugees may feel isolated and unsupported in their new community, which can make it harder for them to navigate the public housing system.

Design Solutions

Below you will find details on my proposed solutions, helping to bridge the gap with public sector decision-makers and users, with a specific focus on public housing schemes for the refugee community.

Digital Kiosk

My initiative focuses on developing a user-friendly digital kiosk in public libraries to enhance refugee engagement with public housing schemes. This platform will enable refugees to complete documentation, research laws and regulations, and provide valuable feedback on their housing experiences.

Find out more

Community Ambassador

My proposal involves recruiting community ambassadors from refugee populations to act as liaisons between decision-makers and their communities, fostering transparency and accountability in public service communication.
